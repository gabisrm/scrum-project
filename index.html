The Pangolin

WHAT IS A PANGOLIN? Pangolins are insect-eating mammals covered in tough, overlapping scales.

Habitat

Where do we live?

Pangolins can be found across Asia and sub-Saharan Africa in sandy areas of woodlands and savannas. Their homes, underground burrows, are usually near water sources.

Diet

Pangolins are insectivorous

Most of their diet consists of various species of ants and termites and may be supplemented by other insects, especially larvae. They are somewhat particular and tend to consume only one or two species of insects, even when many species are available to them. A pangolin can consume 140 to 200 grams (5 to 7 ounces) ofinsects per day.Pangolins are an important regulator of termite populations in their natural habitats. Estimates indicate that one adult pangolin can consume more than 70 million insects annually.

Poor vision, good smell, good hearing

Pangolins have very poor vision, so they rely heavily on smell and hearing. Pangolins also lack teeth; therefore they have evolved other physical characteristics to help them eat ants and termites. Their skeletal structure is sturdy and they have strong front legs that are useful for tearing into termite mounds. They use their powerful front claws to dig into trees, ground, and vegetation to find prey, then proceed to use their long tongues to probe inside the insect tunnels and to retrieve their prey. They also have special muscles in their mouths which prevent ants and termites from escaping after capture.

Their saliva is sticky

The structure of their tongue and stomach is key to aiding pangolins in obtaining and digesting insects. Their saliva is sticky, causing ants and termites to stick to their long tongues when they are hunting through insect tunnels. Without teeth, pangolins also lack the ability to chew; however, while foraging, they ingest small stones (gastroliths) which accumulate in their stomachs to help to grind up ants. [34] This part of their stomach is called the gizzard, and it is also covered in keratinous spines. These spines further aid in the grinding up and digestion of the pangolin's prey. Some species, such as the tree pangolin, use their strong, prehensile tails to hang from tree branches and strip away bark from the trunk, exposing insect nests inside.

Mating and reproduction

Males are larger than females, weighing up to 40% more. While the mating season is not defined, they typically mate once each year, usually during the summer or autumn. Rather than the males seeking out the females, males mark their location with urine or feces and the females will find them. If there is competition over a female, the males will use their tails as clubs to fight for the opportunity to mate with her.

Gestation periods differ by species, ranging from roughly 70 to 140 days. African pangolin females usually give birth to a single offspring at a time, but the Asiatic species may give birth to from one to three. Weight at birth is 80 to 450 g (2 3 ⁄ 4 to 15 3 ⁄ 4 oz) and the average length is 150 mm (6 in). At the time of birth, the scales are soft and white. After several days, they harden and darken to resemble those of an adult pangolin. During the vulnerable stage, the mother stays with her offspring in the burrow, nursing it, and wraps her body around it if she senses danger. The young cling to the mother's tail as she moves about, although in burrowing species, they remain in the burrow for the first two to four weeks of life. At one month, they first leave the burrow riding on the mother's back. Weaning takes place around three months of age, at which stage the young begin to eat insects in addition to nursing. At two years of age, the offspring are sexually mature and are abandoned by the mother.

Scientific Classification

There are eight species of pangolin – four species in Africa and four species in Asia. These eight species comprise their very own Order: Pholidota and one extant Family, Manidae.

Asian pangolins:

  • Chinese pangolin (Manis pentadactyla)
  • Sunda pangolin (Manis javanica)
  • Indian pangolin (Manis crassicaudata)
  • Philippine pangolin (Manis culionensis)

African pangolins:

  • Cape or Temminck's Ground pangolin (Smutsia temminckii)
  • White-bellied or Tree pangolin (Phataginus tricuspis)
  • Giant Ground pangolin (Smutsia gigantea)
  • Black-bellied or Long-tailed pangolin (Phataginus tetradactyla)

Animal Anatomy and Biology

General size information

The dimensions of the pangolins vary between the smallest species, the long-tailed pangolin (which measures 30-40 cm, but has a tail of between 60-70 cm) 6 and the largest giant pangolin (75-85 cm and a tail of 65-80 cm). A giant pangolin specimen reached a weight of 33 kg. In general, females are smaller than males. Females are generally smaller. The external appearance of pangolins is characterized by the long, stiff, plaque-like scales that cover its body, which is an unusual attribute among mammals. They are often compared to walking artichokes or pineapples. They can be rolled into a ball when threatened, with their scales overlapping acting as armor. 2.

Average life

The longest time a giant pangolin has lived in captivity is four years, roughly the same lifespan of other captive pangolin species. The longest record of a pangolin (unknown species) lifespan in captivity is 12-13 years, but this record is unusual. Health complications are the main causes of mortality. Too much interaction and disturbance by humans, especially during the day, has been shown to significantly increase stress levels in captive pangolins. These high levels of stress can compromise your immune systems and make you more susceptible to illness and infection. The causes of death range from gastrointestinal diseases, pneumonia, parasites, and skin diseases. Their digestive systems are highly specialized for a diet high in fat, protein, and calories that can be difficult to imitate with artificial foods.

The lifespan and common cause of death in nature, excluding human hunting, of giant pangolins are unknown. Given their observed low reproductive rate of only one offspring per gestation, their natural life may be long, but more research is required

Physical characteristics

Pangolins have a small, conical and elongated skull, one of the simplest of all mammals. Although their ears are very small or absent, 7 pangolins have a well-developed ear. His sense of smell is also well developed; instead, they have quite poor eyesight. Pangolins are characterized by presenting large, hardened scales in the form of plates that cover the entire body except the muzzle, face, belly and the inside of the limbs. These plates are soft on newborn pangolins, but harden as the animal grows.8 The plates are made of keratin, the same material that human nails, animal claws, or animal horns are made of. rhinos. For this reason, pangolins have the appearance of a walking pineapple or artichoke. When threatened, pangolins are rolled into a ball, using the plates as a form of armor and hiding the face under the tail. The defensive function of these scales is reinforced by the fact that they are very sharp and that the animal can control it by means of special muscles, and can use them to hurt any animal that tries to put the snout or paw inside the armor.9 Its tongue is extraordinarily long and muscular; it arises from the thorax between the sternum and the trachea, in the chest of the animal. As a result, the tongue and associated muscles are longer than the head and body, allowing the pangolin to stretch its tongue in surprising ways. Pangolins lack teeth. Instead, the pyloric area of ​​his stomach is thick and muscular, with projections of keratinous spines towards his interior. It usually contains pebbles, which it uses to crush food.

They have short legs, with sharp claws that they use both to dig in termite and ant nests and to climb trees. They are robust legs, and the later ones are longer than the front ones. The claws of the front legs are so long that they are not well adapted for walking, so pangolins walk with the front legs shrunk to protect them. For this reason, pangolins move quite slowly, although in some cases they can only run with the hind legs, maintaining balance through the tail and reaching speeds of 5 km / h.11 They can also release a smelly acid of glands close to the anus, in a similar way to skunks
